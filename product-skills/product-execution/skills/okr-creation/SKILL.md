# OKR Creation

## Purpose
Write OKRs that drive meaningful outcomes — not just activity, shipping, or vanity metrics.

## OKR Anatomy

### Objective
What do we want to achieve? (Qualitative, inspiring, directional)
- Ambitious but achievable
- Time-bound (usually quarterly)
- No numbers in the Objective itself
- Answers: "What do we want to be true at the end of this quarter?"

Good: "Become the onboarding experience users rave about"
Bad: "Improve onboarding" (too vague) or "Ship onboarding redesign" (output, not outcome)

### Key Results
How do we know we've achieved the Objective? (Quantitative, measurable)
- 2-5 per Objective
- Each KR must move for the Objective to be achieved
- Specific number with baseline and target
- Not a task or milestone ("complete X" is a task, not a KR)

Good KR: "Increase 7-day activation rate from 34% to 55%"
Bad KR: "Launch onboarding redesign" (task, not outcome)
Bad KR: "Improve onboarding metrics" (not measurable enough)

## OKR Hierarchy

**Company OKRs** → align with company's annual strategy
**Team OKRs** → ladder up to company OKRs; owned by a specific team
**Individual OKRs** → optional; owned by individuals, ladder to team OKRs

Rule: Every team OKR should trace to at least one company OKR.

## Stretch vs Committed OKRs

**Committed OKRs**: Must achieve. Team arranges work to hit these. 70% achievement = failure.
Examples: SLA, contractual, financial

**Aspirational/Stretch OKRs**: Aim high. 70% achievement = success. 100% = you set it too easy.
Examples: Growth, innovation, product quality

## OKR Quality Checklist
For each set of OKRs:
- [ ] Objective is inspiring, not a task
- [ ] Each KR is measurable (specific number)
- [ ] Each KR has a baseline (where are we now?) and target (where do we want to be?)
- [ ] Achieving all KRs would definitely mean Objective is achieved
- [ ] You can't achieve the Objective without the KRs (necessary, not just sufficient)
- [ ] Team has influence over all KRs (not fully dependent on external factors)
- [ ] No more than 3-4 Objectives per team per quarter
- [ ] No more than 4-5 Key Results per Objective

## Health Metrics vs OKRs
OKRs should push for improvement.
Health metrics (baseline performance) should be maintained separately.

Health metric: "Support response time stays under 2 hours"
OKR KR: "Increase first-contact resolution from 62% to 80%"

Don't put health metrics as OKRs — they dilute focus.

## Common OKR Mistakes
| Mistake | Fix |
|---|---|
| Outputs as KRs | Replace "ship X" with "X% of users doing Y" |
| Too many OKRs | Cut to 3 Objectives × 3 KRs maximum |
| Not connected to strategy | Check: does each Obj ladder to company strategy? |
| Set-and-forget | Review progress weekly, mid-quarter health check |
| Individual performance tied to OKR score | OKRs are team tools, not performance review |

## Output Format
- Full OKR set (Objective + Key Results with baselines and targets)
- Ladder map to company OKRs
- Committed vs stretch designation
- Review cadence recommendation
